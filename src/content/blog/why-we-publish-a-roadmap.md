If you've ever worked with an SEO agency, you've probably had this experience: you're told things are "in progress." New strategies are "being developed." Improvements are "in the pipeline." You nod along, because what else can you do? There's no artifact you can point to. No checklist. No public record of what's been delivered and what hasn't.

We publish a feature roadmap. It's on our website. Anyone can look at it.

## What's on it

The roadmap is organized into three columns: shipped, building, and planned.

**Shipped** means it's live and running. Right now that includes daily technical audits, GA4 organic funnel snapshots, link prospecting and outreach emails, content intelligence from trending sources, rank tracking against named competitors, and a full action log that records every automated action taken on your behalf.

**Building** means it's actively in development. Currently that's backlink monitoring (tracking new and lost links on a schedule) and SERP feature detection (identifying which search features your competitors hold that you don't).

**Planned** means we've scoped it but haven't started building yet. That includes a brand mention monitor and automated schema auditing.

Every item on the roadmap is a real capability with a defined scope. It's not a vague promise. It's software that either exists or is being written.

## Why this matters

The obvious answer is accountability. If we say we're going to build backlink monitoring, you can check back next month and see if we did. That's a level of commitment most agencies actively avoid, because it creates a record they can be measured against.

But the deeper reason is that publishing a roadmap signals something about how we think. Agencies sell services. We're building a product. Services are defined by hours and deliverables. Products are defined by capabilities and features. When you hire an agency, you're buying someone's time. When you use a product, you're buying what it can do.

That distinction matters because products get better. Every feature shipped makes the platform more capable. Backlink monitoring doesn't just help the client who asked for it. It helps every client, permanently. The investment compounds.

## Transparency as competitive advantage

Most agencies operate as black boxes. You send them money, they send you a report. What happens in between is opaque. Sometimes that opacity is deliberate, because the actual work wouldn't justify the fee if clients could see it. Sometimes it's just organizational inertia. Nobody thought to make the process visible.

We think transparency is a competitive advantage precisely because so few agencies practice it. When a prospective client compares us to a traditional agency, they can see exactly what they'd be getting. Not "SEO services." Not "monthly optimization." A specific list of capabilities that run on a defined schedule, with a public record of what's been built and what's coming next.

That might not matter to everyone. Some businesses just want to hand off SEO and not think about it. That's fine. But for the kind of client who asks hard questions about what they're actually paying for, a published roadmap answers most of them before the first call.

## The underlying bet

Publishing a roadmap is a bet that being specific and honest about what you do is better than being vague and flexible. It means we can't hide behind ambiguity. If something's not on the roadmap, we're not doing it. If something's in the "shipped" column, it better actually be running.

That constraint is the point. It forces us to be deliberate about what we build and honest about what we haven't built yet. And it gives clients a shared language to talk about what they need. Instead of "can you do more link building?" it becomes "when is backlink monitoring moving from building to shipped?"

That's a better conversation for everyone.
